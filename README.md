# Marine Snow Acceleration

Marine Snow Acceleration is an open research program for ocean carbon sequestration.

The project asks whether the ocean’s natural carbon pump can be improved by engineering the export pathway: the fall of carbon-rich marine material from the surface ocean into deep-ocean reservoirs.

The central doctrine is:

> Engineer the fall, not the bloom.

The project does not treat surface productivity, algal growth, visible blooms, or gross carbon uptake as sufficient evidence of sequestration. The governing metric is:

> Net additional durable carbon stored at depth after ecological, chemical, operational, and lifecycle costs are counted.

## Candidate Pathways

This repository treats multiple pathways as candidates, including:

- engineered coccolithophores,
- aggregation enhancement,
- bio-ballasting,
- zooplankton packaging,
- mineral-assisted flocculation,
- in situ sargassum ballasting,
- hybrid biological-mineral systems.

No pathway defines the project. Each pathway must prove itself against the same carbon-accounting, ecological, and governance standards.

## In Situ Sargassum Ballasting

One concrete pathway is the direct treatment of floating pelagic sargassum over sufficiently deep water.

The concept is to apply a marine-safe ballast formulation that clings to floating sargassum, increases its density, collapses buoyancy, and causes it to sink before it reaches shore.

This pathway may align climate goals with coastal-management needs in regions affected by large sargassum arrivals, including the Caribbean and Yucatán Peninsula.

The project does not assume that sinking sargassum is automatically carbon sequestration. It must prove:

- the treated biomass sinks reliably,
- the carbon reaches sufficient depth,
- decomposition products do not rapidly return to the atmosphere,
- lifecycle emissions do not erase the benefit,
- ecological impacts are measured and bounded,
- avoided coastal harm is real,
- monitoring and verification are credible.

## Repository Purpose

This repository exists to organize open research, assumptions, models, design documents, risks, and experimental proposals.

It is not a deployment authorization.

It is not a claim that ocean intervention is risk-free.

It is not a carbon-crediting project.

It is a structured inquiry into whether marine carbon-export pathways can be responsibly improved.

## First Principle

The project exists to increase verified net durable carbon storage in the deep ocean, without pretending that growth is sequestration, sinking is permanence, or intervention is innocence.
