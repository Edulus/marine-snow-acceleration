# Sargassum Signal — Initial Editorial Calendar

## Purpose

This document defines the first publishing sequence for Sargassum Signal.

The goal is to launch with a clear educational arc before moving into weekly map briefs and policy-facing content.

## Launch Arc

### Article 1 — The Great Atlantic Sargassum Belt Is Not a Blob

**Purpose:** Correct the common misconception that the GASB is one continuous mat.

**Key points:**

- It is patchy, dynamic, and seasonal.
- Satellite products show concentration patterns, not a single island.
- This matters for interception and MRV.

### Article 2 — Why Caribbean Beaches Are Being Buried in Seaweed

**Purpose:** Explain the coastal problem without treating all sargassum as waste.

**Key points:**

- Offshore habitat can become nearshore hazard.
- Beaching affects tourism, odor, water quality, waste systems, and public health.
- Mexico / Quintana Roo is a key case.

### Article 3 — Sargassum Is Habitat Before It Becomes a Hazard

**Purpose:** Establish ecological seriousness.

**Key points:**

- Floating sargassum supports marine life.
- The target cannot be “all sargassum.”
- The target is forecasted beach-bound nuisance biomass.

### Article 4 — The Case for Offshore Interception

**Purpose:** Introduce the core operational idea.

**Key points:**

- Beach cleanup is late intervention.
- Offshore interception may prevent landfall.
- Forecasting and drift modeling are central.

### Article 5 — Why Beach Cleanup Is the Worst Time to Solve the Problem

**Purpose:** Make the logistics argument.

**Key points:**

- Wet, decomposing biomass is expensive to handle onshore.
- Landfill, transport, odor, and disposal create costs.
- Interception reframes the problem upstream.

### Article 6 — Can Sargassum Be Compressed Into Sinking Bales?

**Purpose:** Introduce the engineering question.

**Key points:**

- Fresh sargassum floats.
- Compression may help but is unproven.
- The first experiment is compression-only sinking.

### Article 7 — How Deep Is Deep Enough?

**Purpose:** Explain depth, residence time, and no-sink zones.

**Key points:**

- 1,000 m is a working floor, not a magic number.
- 2,000–3,000 m is preferred for pilots.
- Shelves and reefs are no-sink zones.

### Article 8 — Why Mexico and Quintana Roo Matter

**Purpose:** Introduce the coastal-protection branch.

**Key points:**

- Cancún, Tulum, Holbox, Cozumel, and Riviera Maya are receptors.
- The funding case is avoided beaching first, carbon research second.
- Mexico-specific maps and Spanish-language outreach are needed.

### Article 9 — What Would Count as Proof?

**Purpose:** Explain MRV.

**Key points:**

- Sinking is not storage.
- Avoided beaching requires counterfactual evidence.
- Carbon claims require mass, depth, residence time, emissions, and uncertainty.

### Article 10 — The Sargassum Sinking Question

**Purpose:** Tie the series together.

**Key points:**

- This is a research question, not a deployment claim.
- It could fail.
- But if tested responsibly, it could matter.

## Recurring Weekly Format

### Weekly Sargassum Map Brief

Suggested structure:

1. Map source and date.
2. What the satellite products appear to show.
3. Caribbean / Gulf / Mexico notes.
4. What cannot be inferred yet.
5. Possible implications for interception research.
6. One technical note.

## Monthly Format

### Monthly Caribbean Sargassum Outlook

Suggested structure:

1. Month in context.
2. Distribution pattern.
3. Likely coastal-risk areas.
4. Mexico / Quintana Roo section.
5. Research or policy update.
6. Open questions.

## Publishing Rhythm

Initial launch rhythm:

- 2 foundational articles per week for five weeks, or
- 1 foundational article per week for ten weeks.

After launch:

- weekly map brief,
- monthly outlook,
- one deeper article every 2–4 weeks.
