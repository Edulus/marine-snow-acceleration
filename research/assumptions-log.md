# Assumptions Log

## Purpose

This file records project assumptions so they can be challenged, revised, or retired.

Every important assumption should state:

- status,
- why it matters,
- what would support it,
- what would falsify it.

## Assumptions

### A-001: Sargassum is already a costly coastal-management problem

**Status:** Plausible; needs quantified sources.

**Why it matters:** The coastal-management case partly depends on avoided beaching, odor, cleanup, tourism disruption, nearshore decomposition, and public-health burdens.

**Evidence that would support it:** Peer-reviewed estimates, government cleanup costs, tourism revenue impacts, public-health reports, and satellite-observed landfall events.

**Evidence that would weaken it:** Low or inconsistent cost estimates, successful cheaper alternatives, or evidence that target offshore biomass would not have caused harm.

### A-002: Some offshore sargassum can be identified as likely to cause coastal harm

**Status:** Uncertain; requires drift modeling and validation.

**Why it matters:** Treating sargassum that would not have caused coastal harm weakens the avoided-harm claim.

**Evidence that would support it:** Validated drift forecasts, historical trajectory matching, satellite-to-landfall correlation, and treated-versus-untreated comparison mats.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Forecasts cannot reliably distinguish harmful from harmless mats.

### A-003: Spray-based direct ballast treatment can sink floating sargassum

**Status:** Unproven; requires laboratory and controlled-water testing.

**Why it matters:** Spray-based in situ ballasting depends on adhesion, density gain, and low wash-off.

**Evidence that would support it:** Bench and tank tests showing reliable adhesion, negative buoyancy, sinking velocity, low toxicity, and low turbidity.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Ballast washes off, treated mats remain buoyant, or required dose is impractically high.

### A-004: Compression can collapse sargassum buoyancy

**Status:** Unproven; high-priority test.

**Why it matters:** If compression alone makes sargassum sink, mobile densification may avoid external ballast, binders, and chemical dispersal.

**Evidence that would support it:** Compressed sargassum pucks, briquettes, pellets, bales, or mats sink reliably in controlled tests and remain submerged.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Compressed biomass re-expands, re-floats, fragments in the upper water column, or cannot be processed without excessive energy.

### A-005: Mobile processing vessels can operate with acceptable energy and emissions

**Status:** Unknown; requires engineering model.

**Why it matters:** Vessel fuel and processing energy may erase the carbon benefit.

**Evidence that would support it:** Energy-per-ton estimates that remain small relative to durable carbon stored, especially under conservative accounting.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Processing and vessel emissions exceed or nearly equal net durable carbon benefit.

### A-006: Fauna-exclusion intake is feasible

**Status:** Unknown; requires ecological and mechanical design review.

**Why it matters:** Sargassum mats are habitat. Mobile densification vessels may harm organisms living in or around the mats.

**Evidence that would support it:** Intake designs that reduce non-target organism intake, camera monitoring, low intake speeds, escape paths, and shutdown protocols.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** High unavoidable intake of fish larvae, invertebrates, turtles, birds, or other organisms.

### A-007: Sinking over sufficient depth can increase carbon residence time

**Status:** Plausible; requires oceanographic modeling.

**Why it matters:** Depth alone is not permanence. Climate benefit depends on how long decomposition products remain isolated from the atmosphere.

**Evidence that would support it:** Residence-time models, tracer studies, and region-specific circulation analyses showing slow atmospheric return.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Rapid upwelling, shallow remineralization, or fast return pathways in candidate treatment zones.

### A-008: Treated biomass will not re-float after sinking begins

**Status:** Unknown; requires decomposition and buoyancy tests.

**Why it matters:** Re-floating would undermine carbon-storage claims and could create new surface or coastal impacts.

**Evidence that would support it:** Long-duration tank tests showing processed units remain submerged during decomposition.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Gas accumulation, expansion, or fragmentation causes processed biomass to return to the surface.

### A-009: Deep-ocean impacts can be bounded

**Status:** Unproven; requires ecological assessment.

**Why it matters:** Carbon benefit does not erase ecological responsibility. Benthic smothering, oxygen demand, and microbial shifts must be measured.

**Evidence that would support it:** Models and experiments showing limited deposition footprints, acceptable oxygen demand, and manageable benthic effects.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Large oxygen depletion, sensitive-habitat impacts, broad benthic smothering, or unacceptable ecological disruption.

### A-010: Localized benthic impacts may be acceptable if climate and coastal-protection benefits are large enough

**Status:** Ethical/governance claim; requires explicit review.

**Why it matters:** The project must compare climate harm, coastal sargassum harm, and deep-ocean intervention harm without assuming any of them away.

**Evidence that would support it:** Transparent decision framework, public review, independent ecological assessment, and clearly bounded impacts relative to verified benefits.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Public rejection, legal prohibition, disproportionate ecological impact, or weak carbon benefit.

### A-011: Sargassum pathways can outperform land-based alternatives in some contexts

**Status:** Unknown; requires comparative analysis.

**Why it matters:** Sinking should be compared against compost, biochar, anaerobic digestion, construction materials, landfill, and no-action baselines.

**Evidence that would support it:** Lifecycle analysis showing higher net durable carbon benefit and/or lower total harm than alternatives in specific regions.

**Evidence that would falsify it:** Land-based alternatives provide better carbon, economic, or ecological outcomes.
