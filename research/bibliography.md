## Great Atlantic Sargassum Belt and Sargassum Distribution

### Wang et al. 2019 — The Great Atlantic Sargassum Belt

**Type:** Peer-reviewed article.

**Citation lead:** Mengqiu Wang, Chuanmin Hu, Brian B. Barnes, Gary Mitchum, Brian Lapointe, and Joseph P. Montoya, “The great Atlantic Sargassum belt,” *Science*, 2019.

**Use in project:** Foundational source for the existence, satellite detection, and recurrence of the Great Atlantic Sargassum Belt.

**Notes:** Source-check required for exact lat/lon boundaries and biomass estimates before those values are used in operational planning.

**Link:** https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaw7912

### USF / Optical Oceanography Lab Sargassum Watch

**Type:** Monitoring/data source.

**Use in project:** Candidate data source for satellite-based sargassum detection and monthly/near-real-time bloom monitoring.

**Notes:** Should be used as a data source for operational zone selection and MRV rather than as a static map.

**Link:** https://optics.marine.usf.edu/projects/saws.html

### Beron-Vera et al. 2022 — Dynamical geography and transition paths of Sargassum

**Type:** Modeling / preprint research lead.

**Use in project:** Supports the need for drift modeling and suggests recurrent high-density probability patterns in the tropical Atlantic. The abstract describes probability density resembling the GASB between roughly 5–10°N.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.06290

### Andrade-Canto et al. 2021 — Carriers of Sargassum and coastal inundation

**Type:** Modeling / preprint research lead.

**Use in project:** Supports the role of mesoscale eddies and coherent transport structures in coastal inundation risk. Relevant to avoided-coastal-harm evidence and treatment-zone targeting.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.02522

### Beron-Vera et al. 2026 — Tracing the origin of tropical North Atlantic Sargassum blooms to West Africa

**Type:** Modeling / preprint research lead.

**Use in project:** Research lead on West African source-region hypotheses and long-range tropical Atlantic transport.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.04866

### Axios / USF researcher quote on patchiness

**Type:** Media summary with expert quote.

**Use in project:** Supports the operational framing that the belt is not a single continuous “blob,” but many distributed patches.

**Notes:** Useful for communication and operational framing; prefer USF primary data for formal work.

**Link:** https://www.axios.com/local/tampa-bay/2023/03/23/seaweed-belt-florida-research

## Depth, Residence Time, Marine Snow, and Carbon Storage

### Marine snow and deep-ocean residence time

**Type:** General reference / research lead.

**Use in project:** Supports the basic concept that carbon transported into the deep ocean can remain isolated from the atmosphere over long circulation timescales.

**Notes:** Requires stronger primary-source support before numerical storage claims are made.

**Link:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_snow

### Particulate organic carbon remineralization below 1000 m

**Type:** Research lead.

**Use in project:** Supports the working rule that particulate organic carbon remineralized below roughly 1000 m may have century-scale sequestration relevance.

**Notes:** The 1000 m threshold should remain a working threshold, not a universal rule. It depends on water mass, circulation, upwelling, and decomposition depth.

**Link:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_P_%28ocean_measurement_site%29

### Abyssal plain depth ranges

**Type:** General bathymetry/geology reference.

**Use in project:** Supports the general statement that abyssal plains are commonly 3,000–6,000 m deep.

**Notes:** Does not by itself verify that particular sargassum patches overlie 5,000 m water. Candidate zones must be checked with bathymetry datasets such as GEBCO.

**Link:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abyssal_plain

### Carbonate compensation depth

**Type:** General ocean chemistry reference.

**Use in project:** Supports distinction between organic carbon sinking and carbonate dissolution. In the Atlantic, CCD is often around ~5,000 m, but varies by basin and water mass.

**Notes:** Important mainly if calcium carbonate or limestone ballast is used; not a sequestration threshold for organic sargassum carbon.

**Link:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonate_compensation_depth

## Governance and Legal Constraints

### London Convention and London Protocol

**Type:** Governance/legal framework.

**Use in project:** Governs deliberate disposal at sea and is relevant to ocean biomass sinking and marine geoengineering review.

**Notes:** Need primary IMO documents for current status of biomass sinking, marine geoengineering amendments, and any post-2024/2025 decisions. Treat commercial-scale deployment as legally uncertain until reviewed by counsel and competent authorities.

**Link:** https://www.imo.org/en/OurWork/Environment/Pages/London-Convention-Protocol.aspx

### Marine geoengineering under London Convention / Protocol

**Type:** Governance/legal overview.

**Use in project:** Supports the cautious claim that marine geoengineering activities are under international scrutiny and that the 2013 London Protocol amendment has not fully entered into force.

**Notes:** Secondary summary; should be replaced or supplemented with official IMO meeting documents.

**Link:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoengineering
