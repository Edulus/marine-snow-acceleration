# Mexico / Quintana Roo Coastal Protection Branch

## Purpose

This document defines a specific branch of Marine Snow Acceleration focused on reducing sargassum landfall impacts on the Mexican Caribbean, especially Cancún, Tulum, Holbox, Playa del Carmen, Cozumel, and the wider Quintana Roo coast.

This branch treats sargassum sinking not only as a carbon-sequestration pathway, but also as a coastal-protection service for jurisdictions already paying the economic, ecological, public-health, and tourism costs of sargassum inundation.

## Status

Initial branch framework.

This document is a planning hypothesis, not a funding proposal, deployment plan, or legal conclusion.

## Core Thesis

> If a patch of pelagic sargassum is likely to wash up on the beaches of Quintana Roo, then intercepting, compressing, and sinking it offshore over suitable deep water may provide two measurable benefits: avoided coastal harm and additional durable carbon storage.

For Mexico and Quintana Roo, the near-term value proposition may be stronger as **avoided beaching** than as carbon removal alone.

Carbon storage remains the governing metric for climate claims. Coastal protection becomes an additional public-sector value stream.

## Why Quintana Roo Matters

The Mexican Caribbean is one of the clearest potential public-sector partners because:

- sargassum landfall is already a visible public problem,
- tourism-dependent beaches create a strong avoided-cost case,
- Cancún, Tulum, Playa del Carmen, Cozumel, and Holbox are internationally recognized affected destinations,
- federal and state authorities already have incentives to reduce beaching,
- offshore interception may be easier to justify when it prevents known coastal harm,
- funding can be framed as coastal protection, tourism resilience, environmental management, and climate research.

Potential public-sector funders or partners include:

- Government of Mexico,
- Government of Quintana Roo,
- relevant federal environmental agencies,
- maritime and naval authorities,
- tourism and coastal-management agencies,
- municipalities affected by beaching,
- hotel-zone or tourism-sector coalitions,
- Mexican research institutions.

Specific agency names, funding programs, and legal authorities require source-checking.

## Distinction From the General Atlantic Branch

The general Atlantic branch asks:

> Where do sargassum density, deep water, residence time, MRV, and legal permission overlap?

The Mexico / Quintana Roo branch asks a more specific question:

> Which sargassum patches are likely to beach on Quintana Roo, and can they be intercepted and sunk before landfall in a way that is legal, measurable, ecologically bounded, and carbon-accountable?

This branch is therefore counterfactual-driven.

The project must show not only that biomass was sunk, but that the treated biomass plausibly would otherwise have contributed to coastal harm.

## Operational Concept

The Mexico / Quintana Roo branch uses a forecast-and-intercept model:

```text
satellite detection
→ drift forecast
→ identify patch likely to beach on Quintana Roo
→ confirm patch is treatable and not high-value habitat
→ route mobile baler/compressor vessel
→ process sargassum over approved deep-water zone
→ release compressed biomass
→ verify sinking
→ estimate avoided coastal harm
→ estimate durable carbon storage
```

This is not a beach-cleanup operation.

It is an offshore prevention operation.

## Design Doctrine

### 1. Avoided Beaching Is a Required Claim

For this branch, the project should only treat sargassum patches where there is credible drift evidence that the biomass is likely to affect Quintana Roo if untreated.

### 2. No Shelf Sinking

Do not sink on the Yucatán shelf, near reefs, near seagrass beds, or in shallow coastal waters.

If target biomass is near shore, the vessel should either:

- reject treatment,
- collect and move only to an approved deep-water release zone if lawful and feasible,
- or use a different management strategy.

### 3. Deep-Water Release Still Governs

The sinking-zone criteria remain mandatory.

No coastal-protection urgency overrides:

- minimum depth,
- residence-time logic,
- ecological safeguards,
- MRV,
- legal authorization.

### 4. Public-Sector Value Is Broader Than Carbon

Mexico and Quintana Roo may care about:

- avoided beach cleanup,
- reduced tourism disruption,
- reduced odor and hydrogen sulfide exposure,
- reduced nearshore oxygen depletion,
- reduced beach and turtle-nesting disturbance from cleanup machinery,
- reduced landfill burden,
- climate benefit if verified,
- scientific leadership.

The funding case should include these co-benefits while keeping carbon accounting honest.

## Candidate Operating Pattern

### Coastal-Receptor Forecasting

Define monitored receptors:

- Cancún hotel zone,
- Riviera Maya corridor,
- Tulum beaches,
- Holbox and northern Quintana Roo,
- Cozumel-facing waters.

For each receptor, build a drift-risk model:

- satellite-detected patch location,
- wind and current forecast,
- estimated time to landfall,
- likely beaching severity,
- uncertainty range.

### Offshore Interception

Treatment vessels operate only when:

- sargassum patch is forecast to beach,
- interception time is sufficient,
- treatment can occur over approved deep water,
- conditions are safe,
- monitoring can be performed,
- the patch is not excluded on ecological grounds.

### Release Zone

Release zones must satisfy `docs/14-sinking-zone-criteria.md`.

For Quintana Roo, a special concern is avoiding shallow shelf and reef systems. The project should identify adjacent deep-water corridors only through GIS analysis and legal review.

## Funding Logic

A Mexico / Quintana Roo proposal should be framed as:

> A research-first offshore sargassum interception and carbon-storage pilot designed to prevent likely beaching events before they reach the Mexican Caribbean.

The funding case has three parts:

### 1. Avoided Coastal Cost

Measure or estimate:

- beach-cleaning costs,
- hotel and tourism impacts,
- municipal waste handling,
- public-health impacts,
- nearshore ecological impacts,
- loss of beach quality.

### 2. Climate Research Benefit

Measure:

- biomass carbon,
- fraction reaching target depth,
- residence-time estimate,
- lifecycle emissions,
- uncertainty-discounted durable storage.

### 3. Institutional Benefit

Position Mexico / Quintana Roo as:

- a leader in sargassum response innovation,
- a host jurisdiction for responsible research,
- a partner in transparent ocean-CDR governance,
- a beneficiary of open monitoring data.

## MRV for Avoided Beaching

This branch needs two MRV chains.

### Carbon MRV

Same as the core project:

- biomass mass,
- carbon fraction,
- release coordinates,
- sinking velocity,
- depth reached,
- residence time,
- lifecycle emissions,
- uncertainty discount.

### Coastal-Harm MRV

Additional metrics:

- forecast probability of beaching,
- expected receptor beach,
- untreated comparison patches,
- observed beaching reduction if possible,
- cleanup avoided,
- waste avoided,
- nearshore decomposition avoided,
- tourism or beach-use impact indicators.

The project must not claim avoided harm merely because biomass was removed from the surface. It must compare against the likely counterfactual fate of the treated patch.

## Risks Specific to the Mexico / Quintana Roo Branch

- Treating biomass that would not have reached Quintana Roo.
- Overclaiming avoided tourism losses.
- Sinking too close to reefs, seagrass, or shelf habitat.
- Confusing beach cleanup with carbon removal.
- Public concern about deliberate ocean sinking.
- Jurisdictional complexity between federal waters, state interests, municipalities, and international obligations.
- Hurricane and storm-season operational risk.
- Weak MRV leading to loss of credibility.
- Perception that the project is dumping waste rather than preventing landfall and measuring carbon.

## Proposal Framing

Strong framing:

> Mexico and Quintana Roo already face recurring sargassum landfall costs. This project investigates whether forecast-driven offshore interception, mechanical densification, and verified deep-water sinking can reduce those costs while producing measurable climate benefit.

Avoid weak or overconfident framing:

- “We will solve the sargassum problem.”
- “We will sequester all this carbon.”
- “The Mexican government will pay.”
- “Deep water makes it safe.”

Preferred framing:

- research-first,
- permit-first,
- Mexico-specific,
- coastal-protection-led,
- carbon-accounting-honest,
- ecologically bounded,
- transparent and measurable.
