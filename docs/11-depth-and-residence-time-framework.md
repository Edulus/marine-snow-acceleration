# Depth and Residence-Time Framework

## Purpose

This document defines how Marine Snow Acceleration should think about whether an ocean location is deep enough for carbon-storage purposes.

The phrase **deep enough** must not mean merely that gas bubbles are unlikely to visibly reach the surface. It must refer to residence time, circulation, remineralization depth, dissolved carbon retention, oxygen demand, and benthic deposition.

## Status

Initial framework.

## Core Principle

> Sinking is not storage. Depth is not permanence. Residence time is the climate-relevant question.

The project must evaluate whether treated biomass and its decomposition products remain isolated from the atmosphere on climate-relevant timescales.

## Why Depth Matters

A sargassum intervention may move carbon downward, but that carbon may still return to the atmosphere if:

- biomass decomposes in the upper ocean,
- dissolved CO2 returns through mixing or upwelling,
- methane or other gases return to surface waters,
- particles fragment and are consumed before reaching deep water,
- currents transport decomposition products into regions of rapid atmospheric exchange.

Therefore, a treatment zone should be evaluated by water-mass behavior, not just seafloor depth.

## Depth Categories

These categories are working definitions, not final standards.

### Surface and Mixed Layer

Carbon remaining in the mixed layer is unlikely to count as durable storage.

Key concern:

- rapid exchange with atmosphere.

### Below Mixed Layer

Export below the mixed layer is better than surface retention, but may still be too shallow for durable storage.

Key concern:

- seasonal or physical return to surface waters.

### Mesopelagic Zone

The mesopelagic zone may provide partial residence-time benefit, but remineralization is often active here.

Key concern:

- carbon may be respired and return on shorter timescales than desired.

### Bathypelagic / Deep Ocean

Deeper waters may provide longer residence times, depending on circulation.

Key concern:

- oxygen demand and benthic impacts must be measured.

### Sediment Contact

Carbon reaching sediments may have longer storage potential, but benthic deposition can create ecological impacts.

Key concern:

- burial versus remineralization and smothering.

## Suitability Criteria for Sargassum Treatment Zones

A candidate treatment zone should be evaluated against:

1. water depth,
2. mixed-layer depth,
3. expected sinking time,
4. current structure,
5. upwelling risk,
6. water-mass residence time,
7. oxygen status,
8. seafloor habitat sensitivity,
9. protected-area status,
10. distance from shore,
11. monitoring feasibility,
12. probability of avoided coastal harm.

## Residence-Time Questions

For each candidate region, ask:

- At what depth does treated biomass decompose?
- How long do decomposition products remain below the mixed layer?
- How long before dissolved carbon could return to surface exchange?
- Are there local or seasonal upwelling pathways?
- Does deposition occur on sensitive benthic habitat?
- Would the carbon have reached deep water naturally without intervention?
- How much additional residence time does the intervention create?

## Gas and Dissolution Questions

The project shall not rely on the absence of visible bubbling as proof of storage.

Questions include:

- Does decomposition produce CO2, methane, or other gases?
- Do gases dissolve under pressure before reaching the surface?
- Where does dissolved carbon remain in the water column?
- What microbes consume or transform the decomposition products?
- What oxygen demand is created?
- Could methane be oxidized before surface return?

## Location Suitability Matrix

| Criterion | Favorable Condition | Concern |
|---|---|---|
| Depth | Deep water below long-residence layers | Shallow shelves or rapid return pathways |
| Currents | Retain products away from surface exchange | Upwelling or rapid mixing |
| Oxygen | Sufficient oxygen reserve | Oxygen minimum or hypoxia risk |
| Benthic habitat | Low sensitivity or dispersive deposition | Sensitive, protected, or unique habitat |
| Sargassum forecast | Likely coastal harm if untreated | Mat unlikely to beach or cause harm |
| Monitoring | Trackable and accessible | Cannot verify sinking or impacts |
| Governance | Permissible and reviewable | Legal uncertainty or public opposition |

## Minimum Claim Standard

A project may not claim durable carbon benefit from depth alone.

At minimum, it must estimate:

1. treatment depth,
2. sinking velocity,
3. decomposition depth,
4. carbon fraction reaching target depth,
5. dissolved-carbon residence time,
6. atmospheric return risk,
7. oxygen impact,
8. benthic footprint,
9. lifecycle emissions,
10. uncertainty range.

## Open Questions

- What depth threshold is appropriate for early experiments?
- Should target depth be defined by meters, residence time, or both?
- How should oceanographic models be validated for treated biomass?
- How should avoided coastal decomposition be compared to deep-water decomposition?
- How should benthic impacts be discounted or weighted against atmospheric carbon harm?
