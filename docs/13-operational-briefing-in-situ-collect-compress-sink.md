## The Concept in One Line

> Intercept pelagic sargassum in the open Atlantic, mechanically process it on-station, compress or ballast it into negative-buoyancy forms, and release it over deep water so its carbon leaves the fast surface cycle.

No towing to shore.

No coastal offload.

No landfall dependency.

The appeal is that it removes two major logistics problems at once:

1. coastal offload of wet, decomposing biomass,
2. long horizontal tow or transport of buoyant biomass.

The trade is that harvesting, processing, densification, release, and measurement must all work on vessels in open water.

## Relationship to Existing Pathways

This concept sits between two earlier sargassum pathways:

1. **Spray-based in situ ballasting** — treat floating mats directly with ballast or binder-mineral formulations.
2. **Mobile in situ densification** — intake, crush, compress, and release sargassum on-station.

This briefing emphasizes the second pathway.

The preferred operating model is:

```text
find sargassum over suitable deep water
→ collect/intake on-station
→ exclude debris and fauna where possible
→ rupture buoyant structures
→ compress and/or bale
→ add ballast only if needed
→ release immediately over deep water
→ verify sinking and residence-time claim
```

## 1. Where the Deep Water Is

The Great Atlantic Sargassum Belt is a broad, patchy, recurring sargassum system extending across the tropical Atlantic toward the Caribbean.

Operational hypothesis:

- Dense mid-belt biomass may often occur above deep Atlantic water.
- Some central Atlantic operating zones may sit above abyssal depths.
- Depth may be less limiting than sinking reliability, legal permission, MRV, and ecological impact.

This does **not** mean any deep-water location is suitable. The project must evaluate:

- bathymetry,
- mixed-layer depth,
- currents,
- upwelling risk,
- water-mass residence time,
- oxygen status,
- benthic habitat,
- protected-area status,
- governance authority,
- monitoring feasibility,
- avoided coastal harm.

### Working Depth Benchmarks

These are planning benchmarks, not final standards:

| Depth | Planning significance |
|---:|---|
| Below mixed layer | Leaves fastest surface exchange, but may not be durable. |
| ~1,000 m | Candidate practical threshold for long-residence storage; requires validation by region. |
| 3,000 m+ | Often deep-ocean water; residence time may be more favorable, depending on circulation. |
| 4,000–5,000 m | Abyssal depths; benthic impact and monitoring become more difficult. |

The project shall not treat depth alone as proof of sequestration.

> Sinking is not storage. Depth is not permanence. Residence time is the climate-relevant question.

## 2. Whether It Actually Sinks

This is the central engineering problem.

Fresh sargassum floats because of gas-filled bladders, trapped air, and mat structure. Mechanical compression may help, but it should not be assumed sufficient until tested.

Three factors govern success:

### Ballast and Buoyancy

Compression may:

- rupture gas bladders,
- expel trapped air,
- compact loose biomass,
- increase effective density.

However, compression alone may fail if:

- gas bladders remain intact,
- trapped air remains,
- compressed biomass rebounds,
- decomposition creates new buoyant gas,
- bales fragment or re-float.

Therefore, the first experimental question is:

> Can mechanical densification alone make sargassum negatively buoyant and keep it submerged?

If not, the next question is:

> What is the minimum added ballast or binder needed?

### Sink Speed Versus Decomposition

A slow-sinking bale may decompose in the upper water column, returning much of its carbon to the fast surface cycle.

The target is not merely sinking eventually. The target is a fast and decisive descent below the relevant surface-return layer.

Working objective:

> descent through the upper ocean should be measured in hours rather than days, if feasible.

This requires empirical testing of:

- sinking velocity,
- output density,
- bale integrity,
- decomposition rate,
- re-float risk,
- carbon loss during descent.

### Depth Threshold

The project should not require seafloor deposition for every carbon claim. Carbon stored as dissolved inorganic carbon below long-residence layers may still be climate-relevant.

However, the claim must specify:

- depth reached,
- fraction of carbon reaching depth,
- residence-time estimate,
- atmospheric return risk,
- benthic footprint if biomass reaches the seafloor.

## 3. Where “Sink Where Found” Works

The attractive operational case is where dense sargassum and deep water coincide.

Potentially favorable zones:

- open-ocean central belt regions,
- western belt regions away from shelves,
- areas where drift forecasts indicate likely coastal harm if untreated,
- areas where bathymetry and residence time support storage.

Potentially unfavorable zones:

- continental margins,
- shallow shelves,
- steep slope zones with uncertain deposition,
- regions of strong upwelling or rapid surface return,
- protected areas,
- high-sensitivity benthic habitats,
- areas where monitoring is infeasible.

Operational rule:

> Sink where found only when “where found” is deep, monitorable, permitted, and oceanographically suitable.

If floating sargassum occurs near shelf edges or shallow zones, a different operational mode may be required:

- do not sink on the shelf,
- continue drift monitoring,
- release only after reaching a suitable deep-water zone,
- or reject treatment.

## 4. Ports: Resupply, Not Offload

If biomass is processed and released at sea, ports are not biomass offload points.

Ports serve as:

- fuel and energy supply,
- water and food resupply,
- crew rotation,
- spare parts,
- maintenance,
- data offload,
- regulatory interface,
- emergency support.

This shifts port selection away from cargo-handling capacity and toward:

- proximity to operating zones,
- vessel service capacity,
- political/regulatory alignment,
- scientific support,
- customs and permitting practicality,
- repair and bunkering capability.

### Candidate Base Logic

The briefing hypothesis proposes a two-zone model:

| Zone | Candidate bases | Planning rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Western zone | Barbados; Martinique; Guadeloupe | Near Caribbean sargassum impact region and western open-ocean belt access. |
| Eastern zone | Cape Verde; Dakar | Near eastern Atlantic belt access and West African margin. |
| Northern support hub | Canary Islands | Strong Atlantic logistics and maintenance capacity, but farther north. |

These are planning candidates, not recommendations. Each requires legal, diplomatic, operational, and oceanographic review.

## 5. The Constraints That Actually Bind

Finding deep water may be easier than making credible carbon claims.

The binding constraints are likely to be:

1. sinking reliability,
2. descent speed,
3. residence-time evidence,
4. legal permission,
5. ecological impact,
6. MRV credibility,
7. vessel energy and emissions,
8. public legitimacy.

### Legal Constraint

Deliberate ocean biomass sinking for carbon sequestration may fall under international marine dumping and marine geoengineering governance, including the London Convention / London Protocol framework.

The near-term realistic pathway is research-first:

- permitted scientific research,
- transparent environmental assessment,
- host-jurisdiction authorization,
- independent review,
- public reporting,
- no commercial-scale deployment claim.

Commercial-scale deployment without a clear permitting pathway should be treated as blocked until proven otherwise.

### Ecological Constraint

Large organic deposition at depth may cause:

- local oxygen drawdown,
- benthic smothering,
- microbial shifts,
- food-web alteration,
- habitat impacts.

Concentrated drops increase risk.

A lower-harm operating plan would likely require:

- distributed release,
- deposition-zone rotation,
- benthic monitoring,
- oxygen monitoring,
- avoidance of sensitive habitats,
- limits on biomass per area.

### Measurement Constraint

The carbon claim requires measurement.

Bales or compressed units are attractive because they can be measured more easily than diffuse floating mats.

Minimum MRV should include:

- biomass mass estimate,
- dry mass and carbon estimate,
- processing energy,
- release coordinates,
- water depth,
- output density,
- sinking velocity,
- re-float observation,
- depth reached,
- decomposition estimate,
- lifecycle emissions,
- uncertainty range.

## 7. Operational Hypothesis

The operational hypothesis is:

> A fleet of vessels operating from island and regional support bases could remain on station in deep-water sargassum zones, process floating sargassum into measured negative-buoyancy units, release them immediately, and verify sinking and storage claims.

This hypothesis fails if:

- compression alone does not work,
- ballast requirements are too high,
- descent is too slow,
- biomass re-floats,
- vessel emissions erase the benefit,
- permitting is unavailable,
- MRV is too weak,
- ecological impacts are unacceptable,
- alternative uses perform better.

## Bottom Line

The central operational insight is sound enough to test:

> Do not tow wet sargassum to land. Process it where it is, over deep water, if and only if the site is suitable and the result can be measured.

But the strongest version of the concept is not the most optimistic version.

The strongest version is research-first:

1. test whether compression alone sinks sargassum,
2. test whether compressed biomass stays down,
3. define what “deep enough” means by residence time,
4. build an MRV evidence chain,
5. design permitted small-scale research,
6. compare against land-based alternatives,
7. scale nothing until the carbon and ecological accounting survive review.
