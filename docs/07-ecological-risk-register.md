# Ecological Risk Register

## Purpose

This document identifies ecological risks and monitoring needs for Marine Snow Acceleration.

It includes risks common to all pathways, plus pathway-specific risks for spray-based in situ sargassum ballasting and mobile in situ sargassum densification.

## Status

Expanded placeholder.

## Risk Doctrine

Carbon is the target.

Ecological integrity is the boundary.

Under climate emergency conditions, localized ecological impacts may be considered only if they are measured, bounded, and clearly outweighed by verified climate and coastal-protection benefits.

The project shall not treat carbon benefit as a license to ignore ecological harm. It shall compare harms explicitly: atmospheric carbon harm, coastal sargassum harm, nearshore decomposition harm, and deep-ocean intervention harm.

## Initial Risk Categories

### Carbon and Accounting Risks

- Carbon underperformance
- Carbonate or chemical penalty
- Upper-ocean decomposition
- Rapid atmospheric return of decomposition products
- Measurement failure
- Lifecycle emissions exceeding carbon benefit
- Premature carbon-crediting

### Ecological Risks

- Plankton disruption
- Food-web disruption
- Deep-ocean oxygen stress
- Benthic smothering
- Benthic community alteration
- Protected species interaction
- Transboundary ecological effects
- Harmful bloom stimulation

### Governance and Legitimacy Risks

- Public opposition
- Weak permitting basis
- Inadequate consultation
- Moral hazard
- Premature commercialization
- Poor transparency

## Spray-Based Ballasting Risks

Spray-based in situ ballasting creates risks related to applied materials.

Risks include:

- ballast pollution,
- binder persistence,
- chemical leaching,
- turbidity plumes,
- wash-off before sinking,
- accidental fertilization,
- carbonate chemistry effects,
- treatment of mats that would not have caused coastal harm,
- inability to distinguish treated from untreated biomass.

## Mobile In Situ Densification Risks

Mobile in situ sargassum densification creates risks related to vessel operation, intake, processing, and release.

Risks include:

- intake of organisms living in sargassum mats,
- intake of fish larvae, invertebrates, turtles, birds, or other non-target organisms,
- debris intake and release,
- vessel strikes or disturbance,
- fuel spills,
- machinery waste,
- noise and light disturbance,
- clogging or malfunction leading to partially processed floating biomass,
- compressed biomass failing to sink,
- compressed biomass fragmenting in the upper ocean,
- compressed biomass re-floating after release,
- high vessel emissions,
- high processing energy demand,
- inaccurate biomass measurement,
- operating outside suitable depth zones,
- repeated concentration of biomass deposition in the same benthic area.

## Compression-Specific Failure Modes

Mechanical densification may fail if:

- crushing does not collapse enough buoyancy,
- gas bladders are not destroyed,
- compressed biomass traps air,
- pucks, pellets, or bales expand after release,
- processed biomass absorbs gas during decomposition and re-floats,
- output units fragment before reaching target depth,
- compression energy requirements are too high,
- wet biomass handling limits throughput,
- machinery clogs under realistic sea states.

## Risk Controls to Investigate

Possible controls include:

- fauna-exclusion intake design,
- slow intake speeds in biologically sensitive areas,
- real-time cameras at intake points,
- debris screening,
- shutdown triggers for protected species,
- operating only in designated deep-water zones,
- rotating deposition zones to avoid repeated benthic loading,
- output-unit density testing,
- re-float testing,
- vessel-emissions accounting,
- independent monitoring.

## Minimum Risk Questions Before Field Testing

Before any open-water test, the project should answer:

1. What non-target organisms inhabit the target sargassum mats?
2. Can the intake system avoid or reduce non-target organism harm?
3. Does compression alone reliably sink sargassum?
4. Does processed biomass remain submerged?
5. What oxygen demand is expected at depth?
6. What benthic area receives the deposited biomass?
7. What is the vessel emission cost per ton processed?
8. What shutdown triggers are required?
9. How will treated biomass be monitored after release?
10. How will deep-ocean impacts be weighed against coastal and climate harms?
