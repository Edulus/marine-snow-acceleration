# Mobile In Situ Sargassum Densification

## Purpose

This document defines the mobile in situ sargassum densification pathway as a distinct engineering and research pathway within Marine Snow Acceleration.

The pathway investigates whether floating pelagic sargassum can be mechanically processed by roaming vessels over sufficiently deep water so that it becomes negatively buoyant and sinks without being transported to shore.

## Status

Initial framework.

This is not a vessel design, deployment plan, or carbon-removal claim. It is a research and concept-engineering document.

## Core Thesis

> Mow, crush, compress, and release sargassum in place over deep water, turning floating nuisance biomass into dense sinking material without transporting it to land.

The vessel is not primarily a transport vessel. It is a mobile in situ processing unit.

Its function is transformation:

```text
floating sargassum
→ intake
→ debris and fauna exclusion where possible
→ crushing / chopping
→ compression / densification
→ immediate release over deep water
→ sinking verification
```

## Why This Pathway Exists

Spray-based in situ ballasting may be able to treat very large mats quickly, but it introduces uncertainties around adhesion, wash-off, chemical release, turbidity, and dosing.

Mobile densification offers a different control strategy. Instead of applying material over floating mats, it mechanically processes known quantities of biomass into measurable output forms.

Potential advantages include:

- no need to transport wet biomass to shore,
- no land-based disposal dependency,
- no external ballast if compression alone works,
- more precise biomass measurement,
- more precise release locations,
- better output-unit tracking,
- lower chemical-additive burden,
- direct comparison between treated and untreated biomass.

Potential disadvantages include:

- vessel emissions,
- machinery energy demand,
- intake harm to organisms living in sargassum,
- clogging,
- weather limits,
- slower throughput than broad-area spraying,
- possible re-float or fragmentation of processed biomass.

## Operating-Zone Logic

Mobile densification should operate only in pre-defined treatment zones that meet all of the following conditions:

1. Sargassum is present in sufficient concentration.
2. The zone is deep enough for meaningful carbon residence time.
3. Drift modeling indicates the biomass may otherwise contribute to coastal harm.
4. The zone is not inside a protected area or otherwise unsuitable ecological region.
5. Vessel operation and monitoring are legally permitted.
6. Baseline observations can be collected.
7. Treated and untreated comparison areas can be defined.
8. Sinking verification is feasible.

The basic operational doctrine is:

> Treat only where the ocean is deep enough, the biomass is likely to matter, and the result can be measured.

## Fleet Concept

A mature fleet could roam designated deep-water treatment zones in repeated patterns.

Possible operating modes include:

- grid mowing,
- drift-line following,
- satellite-guided interception,
- current-edge patrol,
- tourism-protection corridor treatment,
- seasonal high-biomass campaigns,
- adaptive routing based on forecasts and bathymetry.

The fleet should not operate as a blind harvesting system. It should operate as a monitored intervention system with known location, processed biomass estimates, release records, and verification sampling.

## Vessel Process Modules

### 1. Detection and Navigation

The vessel requires a way to locate sargassum and remain inside approved treatment zones.

Possible inputs:

- satellite sargassum detection,
- aircraft or drone reconnaissance,
- onboard visual detection,
- drift forecasting,
- bathymetry maps,
- current forecasts,
- protected-area boundaries.

### 2. Intake / Mower Head

The intake system gathers floating sargassum from the surface.

Research questions:

- What intake speed minimizes non-target organism harm?
- Can the system separate dense sargassum from open water?
- Can intake avoid turtles, birds, fish, and large debris?
- Does the intake work in waves and variable mat density?
- What clogging modes occur?

### 3. Debris and Fauna Exclusion

Sargassum mats are habitat. Intake systems must be designed with fauna exclusion as a first-class requirement.

Possible controls:

- low intake velocities,
- visual or camera-based shutdown,
- coarse debris screening,
- escape gaps,
- manual or automated sorting,
- protected-species shutdown triggers,
- avoidance of high-habitat-value mats where detectable.

### 4. Crushing / Chopping

Crushing targets gas bladders and trapped air.

Chopping may improve compression but could also increase fragmentation and decomposition.

Research questions:

- Does crushing alone eliminate enough buoyancy?
- Does chopping improve or worsen sinking?
- Does smaller particle size increase remineralization during descent?
- What mechanical setting balances density and integrity?

### 5. Compression / Densification

Compression is the central mechanical test.

Possible mechanisms:

- screw press,
- roller press,
- belt press,
- hydraulic press,
- wet extruder,
- briquetting press.

Research questions:

- What compression pressure is required?
- How much water and air are expelled?
- What wet density is achieved?
- Does the output remain compressed after release?
- How much energy is required per wet ton?
- Does compression alone replace ballast?

### 6. Output Formation

Candidate output forms include:

- loose crushed biomass,
- compressed pucks,
- briquettes,
- wet pellets,
- bales,
- compacted mats,
- extruded logs or ropes,
- hybrid biomass-mineral pucks if ballast is needed.

Each output form should be tested for:

- density,
- sinking velocity,
- fragmentation,
- re-float risk,
- decomposition rate,
- handling reliability,
- MRV compatibility.

### 7. Release System

The release system should return processed biomass immediately to the same deep-water treatment zone.

Release records should include:

- timestamp,
- vessel position,
- bathymetric depth,
- output form,
- estimated mass,
- compression setting,
- tagging or tracer information if used.

## First Research Priority

The first technical priority is to test whether compression alone makes sargassum sink.

The hierarchy is:

1. untreated controls,
2. crush-only,
3. chop-only,
4. compress-only,
5. chop + compress,
6. pucks / briquettes / pellets,
7. ballast only if mechanical densification fails,
8. binder only if ballast does not remain integrated.

## Minimum Evidence Before Field Testing

Before any open-water field test, the project should have evidence for:

1. negative buoyancy after processing,
2. sinking velocity sufficient to reach target depth,
3. low re-float risk,
4. acceptable fragmentation behavior,
5. known energy per processed wet ton,
6. vessel-emissions estimate,
7. fauna-exclusion feasibility,
8. release tracking method,
9. treated-versus-untreated comparison design,
10. depth and residence-time suitability.

## Failure Modes

The pathway may fail if:

- compression does not eliminate buoyancy,
- output units re-expand or re-float,
- processed biomass fragments in the upper ocean,
- decomposition occurs before target depth,
- energy demand erases the carbon benefit,
- vessel emissions erase the carbon benefit,
- intake harms ecologically important sargassum-associated organisms,
- machinery clogs at realistic throughput,
- monitoring cannot verify sinking and storage.

## Relationship to Other Pathways

Mobile densification should be compared against:

- spray-based in situ ballasting,
- collection and land-based sargassum management,
- no-action coastal decomposition baseline,
- alternative ocean CDR pathways.

The strongest version of this pathway is not the most aggressive fleet concept. It is the smallest experimentally testable pathway that can falsify or support the central hypothesis:

> Mechanical densification may replace chemical or mineral ballast.
